Context & explanations
👴

Pensions: ~13.8% of GDP — Europe's Fastest-Growing Burden

Spain spent approximately €220 billion on public pensions in 2024 (~13.8% of GDP under the full ESSPROS definition), with old-age pensions alone at 9.7% of GDP per the Eurostat pension map. Contributory pensions were revalued by 3.8% in January 2024 to protect purchasing power. Social Security's pension deficit reached roughly 4% of GDP in 2024, with the state budget transferring tens of billions to plug the gap. The 2021–2023 pension reform repealed the unpopular sustainability factor and re-linked revaluations to CPI, guaranteeing real purchasing power but significantly worsening long-run fiscal projections. Spain's EU Ageing Report forecasts pension spending rising to 16%+ of GDP by 2050 — the steepest increase of any eurozone country.

Pensions: 9.7% GDP old-age (Eurostat map) · ~13.8% ESSPROS total
🌊

Valencia Floods: €6.4B One-Off Costs Added to Deficit

On 29 October 2024, catastrophic flash floods (the DANA storm system) struck the Valencia region, killing over 220 people and causing an estimated €10+ billion in damage. The immediate government response — emergency aid, reconstruction grants, compensation payments, and public infrastructure repair — added approximately €6.4 billion (0.4% of GDP) to the 2024 general government deficit. This was the costliest natural disaster in Spain's modern history. European Commission forecasters noted this impact explicitly. The tragedy also exposed weaknesses in Spain's civil protection and early-warning systems, triggering political controversy and legal investigations into the delayed regional alert response.

Valencia DANA floods: ~€6.4B one-off cost · 0.4% GDP deficit impact
⚖️

Court Rulings: Another €8B in One-Off Expenditure

Spain's 2024 deficit was further impacted by approximately €11.3 billion in court-ordered payments to the general government, of which around €8 billion (0.5% of GDP) was non-recurring. The most significant were rulings on pension supplements for fathers (the "complemento de maternidad"), which the Constitutional Court ruled discriminatory for fathers of large families who were excluded from a benefit previously available only to women. Payments to affected pensioners going back years created a large one-off expenditure spike. These one-offs, combined with flood spending, account for nearly 1% of GDP in exceptional 2024 costs that will not recur fully in 2025.

Court rulings: ~€8B non-recurring · 0.5% GDP · pension supplements
📉

Debt Below 102%: Fastest Decline in Eurozone

Spain's public debt fell from 105.1% of GDP at end-2023 to 101.6% in 2024 — a reduction of 3.5 percentage points, the joint-largest single-year debt decline in the eurozone (alongside Ireland's 3.5 pp drop). This sharp improvement was driven primarily by strong nominal GDP growth of 6.4% (real 3.5% + 2.9% deflator), which outpaced the cost of new borrowing. The deficit of 3.2% would otherwise push debt higher, but the denominator effect of fast GDP growth dominated. Spain's debt is targeted below 100% of GDP by 2026 on current trajectories — a milestone not seen since 2019. Despite this progress, at ~€1.62 trillion, the debt pile remains one of Europe's largest in absolute terms.

Debt: 101.6% GDP · −3.5 pp in 2024 · fastest drop in eurozone
☀️

Tourism Windfall: Record 94M Visitors, 14% of GDP

Spain welcomed approximately 94 million international tourists in 2024 — a new all-time record — generating over €120 billion in receipts. Tourism accounts for roughly 12–14% of GDP and is the primary engine behind Spain's remarkable economic outperformance vs. the eurozone average (3.5% real GDP growth vs. 0.8% EA average). The tourist boom boosted VAT revenues, employment in hospitality, and social contributions across services. Spain's status as the world's second-most-visited country (behind France) provides a structural revenue advantage, though it also fuels housing cost pressures and growing political backlash in cities like Barcelona, Madrid, and the Canary Islands.

Tourism: ~94M visitors · €120B+ receipts · 12–14% of GDP
🏛️

No Budget for Three Years: Governing Without Approval

Spain operated in 2024 — as in 2023 and 2022 — without an approved national budget, after the minority Sánchez government's 2023 budget was rejected in Congress. Under Spanish law (Art. 134 of the Constitution), if no new budget is approved, the previous year's budget is automatically extended (prorrogado). This means the 2021 budget was the last formally approved, creating a structural disconnect between actual spending (which has grown substantially through supplementary credit laws and Decree-laws) and parliamentary authorisation. The Supreme Audit Office (Tribunal de Cuentas) and fiscal watchdog AIReF have both flagged the risks of this prolonged budgetary paralysis for fiscal transparency and multi-year planning.
