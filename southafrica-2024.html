Consolidated vs. main budget: South Africa's "consolidated budget" includes national government, provincial governments, social security funds (UIF, Compensation Fund), and select public entities — but not state-owned enterprises (Eskom, Transnet, etc.). Provincial governments execute ~57% of total spending, primarily on education and health, funded through the provincial equitable share and conditional grants from the national budget. This is why education and health appear so large — they include full national + provincial consolidated spending. National-only main budget expenditure was ~R2.17T in 2024/25.
Context & explanations
🎓

Education: R465B — Largest Consolidated Function

Education receives R465.1B, the single largest functional allocation in the consolidated budget at roughly 20% of all spending. This covers basic education (the largest sub-component, including teacher salaries, school nutrition, and infrastructure), higher education and training (universities and TVET colleges), and skills development. Salaries alone consume 76 cents of every provincial education rand, leaving just 24 cents for everything else — school infrastructure, textbooks, meals. South Africa has consistently struggled with educational outcomes despite high spending: PIRLS and TIMSS international assessments rank SA among the lowest-performing nations. The Funza Lushaka bursary programme funds new teachers, while NSFAS provides means-tested university funding to over 1 million students.

Education: R465B · 20% of total spend · Largest function
💸

Debt Service: R389.6B — 22 Cents of Every Revenue Rand

As Finance Minister Godongwana stated plainly: "Debt service costs amount to R389.6B — 22 cents of every rand we raise in revenue. It is more than what we spend on health, the police and basic education." Gross public debt is heading toward 76% of GDP, the result of fiscal deficits run every year since 2008/09. The good news: FY2024/25 is the first year since 2008/09 that South Africa achieved a primary budget surplus (revenue exceeding non-interest spending), a milestone that should gradually slow debt accumulation. South Africa's debt is overwhelmingly rand-denominated and held domestically, reducing exchange-rate risk but keeping borrowing costs elevated. The National Treasury drew down R150B from the Gold and Foreign Exchange Contingency Reserve Account (GFECRA) to reduce new bond issuance.

Debt service: R389.6B · 22c per revenue rand · Exceeds health + police
🤲

Social Grants: 28 Million Beneficiaries — World's Largest Safety Net per Capita

South Africa's social grant system reaches nearly 28 million people — almost half the population — making it proportionally the largest social safety net among emerging economies. The R296.9B covers the Child Support Grant (18M+ children, R530/month), Old Age Grant (3.9M, R2,180/month), Disability Grant (1.1M, R2,180/month), the SRD Covid-19 Social Relief of Distress Grant (extended at R350/month to ~8M unemployed adults), and various other grants. The SRD grant remains controversial — critics say it's too small to live on and lacks a pathway to employment, while fiscal hawks argue it's unaffordable at scale. With unemployment above 32% and youth unemployment above 60%, the grants have become a structural feature of South African social policy rather than a temporary pandemic measure.

Social grants: R297B · 28M beneficiaries · ~half the population
📈

PIT Dominates Revenue — and the Narrow Tax Base Problem

Personal Income Tax at R733B is South Africa's largest revenue source, contributing 39.5% of total tax revenue. Yet the tax base is extremely narrow: fewer than 7 million individual taxpayers file returns. Only 1.2% of individuals earn above R1.5M yet contribute 30% of all PIT. South Africa's top marginal rate is 45% (above R1.8M), one of the world's highest. The concentration of taxpayers means revenue is fragile — economic or employment shocks in financial services (which accounts for 45% of PAYE) quickly ripple into fiscal shortfalls. The Two-Pot retirement reform (allowing early pension fund access) provided a R7.9B windfall beyond estimates in FY2024/25, but this is a once-off structural effect. The 2024 Budget froze bracket adjustments (a "bracket creep" tax increase) to raise R16.3B.

PIT: R733B · 39.5% of revenue · Fewer than 7M filers
⚡

The Fuel Levy Shock: Loadshedding Ends, Revenue Falls

One of the most striking revenue stories of FY2024/25 is the fuel levy's sharp 6.3% decline to R85.8B — R15.2B below the original budget estimate. The culprit was an unexpected success: South Africa made significant progress in ending "loadshedding" (rolling electricity blackouts). As Eskom's generation improved through 2024, households and businesses that had been running diesel generators switched back to grid power, cutting diesel consumption dramatically. Fuel consumption fell from 24 billion litres to 21 billion litres year-on-year. This perversely punished Treasury, since fuel levies include a general fuel levy (R3.77/L petrol) and Road Accident Fund levy (R2.18/L) — fixed per-litre charges, not ad-valorem taxes. A structural transition to electric vehicles in coming years will further erode this revenue base.

Fuel levy: R85.8B · Down R15.2B · Loadshedding end = less diesel
🏥

Health: R260.7B — National Health Insurance on the Horizon

Health receives R260.7B in consolidated spending, funding a dual-tier system: a large public sector serving roughly 84% of the population with an under-resourced system (poor facilities, staff shortages, equipment failures), alongside a private sector serving the 16% with medical aid insurance and consuming nearly half of total health spend. The National Health Insurance (NHI) Act was signed into law in 2024 — a landmark policy committing South Africa to universal health coverage over an undefined transition period. Implementation faces massive practical challenges: funding the NHI fully would require approximately R200B in additional annual spending at system maturity. The 2024 Budget allocated R1.4B toward NHI groundwork over the medium term — a far cry from what full implementation would cost, suggesting a decades-long transition.

Health: R260.7B · NHI Act signed 2024 · Dual-tier system
