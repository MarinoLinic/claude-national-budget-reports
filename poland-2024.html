Poland's revenue base grew strongly in 2024 on the back of high nominal wage growth (+12% in H1 2024) and near-record employment. Despite this, revenues fell short of budget targets — notably VAT (down PLN 23B), CIT (down PLN 11B) and CO₂ permit sales — forcing a mid-year budget amendment. VAT is the largest single source. Context & explanations
🛡️

Defence: 4.1% of GDP — NATO's #1 Spender

Poland spent approximately PLN 151 billion on defence in 2024 (4.12% of GDP), making it the top NATO member by defence-spending share — surpassing even the United States (3.2%). The surge is a direct response to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Defence spending tripled in absolute terms between 2021 and 2024. Procurements include South Korean K2 main battle tanks, FA-50 fighter jets, HIMARS rocket systems, F-35 fighters, and HK417 rifles. About PLN 62 billion flows through the off-budget Armed Forces Support Fund (FWSZ) via Bank Gospodarstwa Krajowego bonds — a practice criticised by Poland's Supreme Audit Office (NIK) for reducing transparency and understating the headline budget deficit.

Defence: 4.12% GDP · PLN 151B · NATO #1 by GDP share
👶

Family Benefits: 800+, Active Parent & Child Bonus

Poland operates one of the most generous family benefit systems in the EU. The flagship 800+ programme (raised from 500+ in February 2024) pays PLN 800/month per child unconditionally — up from PLN 500 since 2016. The new "Active Parent" (Aktywny Rodzic) programme, launched in October 2024, pays PLN 1,500/month to working parents of children aged 12–35 months. Together, these programmes cost roughly PLN 60–70 billion annually (~1.7% of GDP) and are a major driver of the fiscal deficit. They are also credited with reversing Poland's fertility decline and boosting female labour force participation.

800+ benefit: PLN 800/child/month · universal · ~PLN 60–70B/year
📉

EU's 2nd Highest Deficit — EDP Triggered

Poland's 6.5% general government deficit in 2024 was the second highest in the EU, behind only Romania (9.3%). The EU launched the Excessive Deficit Procedure (EDP) against Poland in July 2024. The deficit results from a combination of record defence spending, large social transfers, 13th and 14th pension payments, a public sector wage increase, and below-target tax revenues. Poland has committed to reducing the deficit below 3% of GDP by 2028–30. The Supreme Audit Office (NIK) noted that the "true" deficit is even larger when off-budget BGK/FWSZ financing is included, reaching close to PLN 240B or 6.6% of GDP under EU methodology.

Deficit: −6.5% GDP · EU's 2nd highest · EDP since July 2024
💰

VAT Shortfall: The 2024 Budget Crisis

VAT is Poland's largest single revenue source, but 2024 collections fell significantly short of budget targets. A near-30% year-on-year increase had been assumed in the original budget — an assumption described as "overly optimistic" by ING analysts. The actual shortfall was PLN 23 billion in VAT alone, alongside PLN 11 billion less in CIT (driven by weak export-oriented firm profits) and PLN 6 billion less from the National Bank's profit transfer. A formal budget amendment in October 2024 raised the state deficit ceiling by PLN 56 billion to PLN 240 billion. Poland is now implementing mandatory e-invoicing (KSeF) from 2026 to close compliance gaps.

VAT shortfall: PLN 23B · CIT miss: PLN 11B · amended Oct 2024
🏥

Healthcare: Chronically Underfunded Despite Reform

Poland spends roughly 6.5% of GDP on healthcare — below the EU average of ~8%. The National Health Fund (NFZ) is financed primarily through a 9% health contribution on gross income, but spending has lagged population needs, resulting in long waiting times for specialist care and elective procedures. The 2024 budget included a PLN 25 billion increase in healthcare funding as part of a medium-term plan to raise health spending to 7% of GDP. Poland's universal public healthcare system is free at the point of use but chronically underfunded, with a dual-tier reality where private insurance and direct payments fill significant gaps.

Healthcare: ~6.5% GDP · NFZ-funded · below EU avg · PLN +25B in 2024
🏗️

Off-Budget Funds: Poland's Transparency Problem

A defining feature of Poland's public finances is the large scale of spending channelled outside the official state budget through the Bank Gospodarstwa Krajowego (BGK). In 2024, over PLN 135 billion of public tasks were financed through BGK-managed funds — equivalent to 16% of state budget expenditure. These funds include the Armed Forces Support Fund, National Road Fund, COVID-19 Prevention Fund, and others. The BGK-related debt alone exceeded PLN 350 billion. NIK (Supreme Audit Office) has criticised this structure for violating budget transparency principles and limiting parliamentary oversight. The Tusk government has pledged to restore budget transparency.

Off-budget BGK funds: >PLN 135B (2024) · ~3.7% GDP · NIK critical
