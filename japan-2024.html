Context & explanations
👴

Social Security: 33.5% of All Spending, Rising Every Year

Social security at ¥37.72T is by far Japan's largest budget line, accounting for a third of all general account expenditure. It rose 2.3% to a record high in FY2024, driven by Japan's rapidly aging population — the oldest in the world, with over 29% aged 65 or more. The category covers pension payments (the largest sub-item), national health insurance subsidies, long-term care insurance, child and family benefits, and employment insurance. Japan's National Health Insurance and Employees' Health Insurance systems are funded through a separate Social Insurance Special Account, meaning the general account figure reflects only the national subsidy top-up, not total healthcare spending. Without structural reform, MoF projects social security will crowd out virtually all other spending within two decades.

Social security: 33.5% of all spending · Record high
⚠️

Bond Dependency at 31.5%: Three Decades of Structural Deficits

Japan has run a general account deficit in every fiscal year since 1975, apart from a brief surplus in FY1990. New bond issuance of ¥35.4T in FY2024 means more than three yen out of every ten spent is borrowed. Japan's outstanding government bond balance reached an estimated ¥1.06 quadrillion (¥1,061T) at the end of FY2024 — roughly 178% of GDP. Paradoxically, Japan has managed this without a debt crisis because roughly 90% of JGBs are held domestically (mainly by banks, insurance companies, and the Bank of Japan), keeping yields near zero for most of the past three decades. However, as the BoJ raises rates toward normalization, servicing this mountain of debt is becoming rapidly more expensive.

Bond dependency: 31.5% · JGB balance ≈ ¥1,061T
🛡️

Defence: Surge to ¥7.9T — Japan's Biggest Rearmament Since WWII

Japan's defence budget surged 16.6% to ¥7.92T in FY2024 (vs ¥6.79T in FY2023), marking the sharpest annual increase since World War II. This is part of a five-year plan adopted in late 2022 to nearly double defence spending to approximately 2% of GDP by FY2027 — from a decades-long self-imposed cap of roughly 1%. The increase funds new missile capabilities, a Tomahawk cruise missile purchase from the US, expanded F-35 fleet, improved cyber and electronic warfare, expanded ammunition stockpiles, and enhanced interoperability with US forces. An additional ¥4.4T of prior-year carry-over funds were deposited into the Defence Buildup Fund to smooth spending over the five-year plan period.

Defence: +16.6% · Five-year doubling plan in progress
🏙️

Local Allocation Tax Grants: Japan's Fiscal Equalisation System

Local Allocation Tax (LAT) grants of ¥17.79T are transfers from the national government to prefectures and municipalities to ensure every local government can provide a baseline of public services regardless of its own tax base. Unlike the US or UK, Japan's local governments have very limited independent revenue-raising powers, making them heavily dependent on central transfers. The LAT system is funded by a fixed legal share of five major national taxes — income tax (33.1%), corporation tax (50%), consumption tax (19.5%), liquor tax (50%), and tobacco tax. In years where these shares fall short, the national government tops up with its own funds, which is counted as general account expenditure. A further ¥3.4T in local transfer taxes (earmarked revenues) flows to localities outside this framework.

LAT grants: ¥17.8T · 15.8% of all spending · Fiscal equalisation
📈

Interest Rates Rising: The Coming Debt-Service Shock

Japan's debt interest payments of ~¥9.6T in FY2024 were kept low by nearly three decades of near-zero interest rates maintained by the Bank of Japan. However, in March 2024 the BoJ abandoned negative interest rates, and in July 2024 raised its policy rate to 0.25%, signalling a return toward normalisation. MoF estimates show that each 1 percentage point rise in interest rates adds approximately ¥3.7T to annual interest spending with a 3-year lag, as maturing bonds are rolled over at higher rates. The FY2025 budget already shows a ¥0.6T increase to ¥10.5T in interest. By FY2026, interest hit ¥13T — a 35% jump in two years. With ¥1.06 quadrillion in outstanding bonds, even modest rate rises translate into enormous additional cost.

Interest now rising: ¥9.6T → ¥13T by FY2026
👶

Children & Birthrate: Record Spending on a Crisis Problem

Japan's total fertility rate reached a record low of 1.20 in 2023, among the lowest in the developed world. In response, the FY2024 budget included ¥5.3T for the Children and Families Agency (up from ¥4.8T in FY2023), covering childcare subsidies, expanded child allowances, parental leave improvements, and fertility treatment support — a 10% year-on-year increase. The government's "Children's Future Strategy" commits to raising combined central and local spending by ¥3.6T over three years. An additional ¥0.2T was raised through the new "Child Special Bond" (子ども特例公債), a novel financing instrument which drew criticism for passing child-rearing costs on to future generations. Japan created a new ¥500B/year dedicated fund for children's policies, financed partly by a small social insurance surcharge.

Children's budget: ¥5.3T · +10% · Fertility crisis response
