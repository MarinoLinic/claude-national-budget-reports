Context & explanations
👴

Pensions: ~16% of GDP — The Eurozone's Heaviest Burden

Italy spends approximately 16% of GDP on public pensions (ESSPROS definition, consistent with the Eurostat pension map showing 11.8% for old-age only), making it the second-highest pension spender in the OECD after Greece. Over 16 million people receive a pension from INPS, including old-age, disability, and survivors' benefits. The 1995 Dini reform introduced a notional defined contribution (NDC) system, but its slow phase-in means mixed-system pensions will persist until the 2040s. At least one quarter of pension spending is not covered by contributions, requiring general-tax top-up. Italy's pension age is linked to life expectancy — currently 67 — but generous "Quota" early-retirement schemes (Quota 103 in 2024) allow retirement as early as 62 with 41 years of contributions.

~16% GDP ESSPROS · 11.8% GDP old-age only (Eurostat map) · 16M+ recipients
🏠

Superbonus: A Fiscal Time Bomb Still Unwinding

The Superbonus 110% scheme — a building renovation tax credit introduced in 2020 that allowed homeowners to claim 110% of costs as transferable tax credits — created a structural distortion in Italy's public finances. By 2024, accumulated credits recognised in national accounts exceeded €220 billion, driving Italy's deficit to 7.2% of GDP in 2023 despite ostensibly strong tax revenues. The fiscal drag from Superbonus unwinding contributed to an unusually high 7.2% deficit in 2023 and continued to affect 2024 accounts. ISTAT's recording of these deferred credits as government expenditure in the year they were granted (2020–2022) rather than when redeemed created sudden large deficit spikes, now gradually correcting.

Superbonus credits: >€220B · deficit spike 7.2% GDP (2023)
💸

Debt: €2,967B — A Near-€3 Trillion Mountain

Italy's gross public debt reached €2,967 billion (134.9% of GDP) at end-2024 — the second largest nominal debt pile in the eurozone after Germany's, but far more problematic relative to GDP. Interest payments cost €85.6 billion (3.9% of GDP), consuming more of the budget than education, defence, and most social programmes combined. The average cost of debt is rising as cheap pre-2022 issuances mature and are refinanced at higher rates. Despite a positive primary balance in 2024 (+0.5% GDP), the debt-to-GDP ratio edged up slightly, because the primary surplus was not enough to outpace nominal interest costs, debt issuance costs, and stock-flow adjustments.

Debt: €2,967B · 134.9% GDP · interest €85.6B (3.9% GDP)
📉

Deficit Halved: From −7.2% to −3.4% in One Year

Italy's headline deficit fell sharply from 7.2% of GDP in 2023 to 3.4% in 2024 — an improvement of nearly 4 percentage points, the largest single-year correction in decades. The primary balance swung from −3.5% to +0.5% of GDP. The improvement was mainly mechanical: most of the 2023 deficit was driven by deferred Superbonus accounting entries that did not recur in 2024, rather than genuine fiscal consolidation. Italy remains in the EU's Excessive Deficit Procedure and has committed to reducing the deficit below 3% of GDP by 2026. The Meloni government's fiscal path relies on expenditure restraint, RRF investment grants, and continued revenue growth from employment and wages.

Deficit: −3.4% GDP · improvement of 3.8 pps from 2023
⚕️

Healthcare: Underfunded at ~6.8% of GDP

Italy's Servizio Sanitario Nazionale (SSN) is a nominally universal public health service, but per-capita health spending is among the lowest in Western Europe at ~€2,700/year. At ~6.8% of GDP, it lags France (11.2%), Germany (12.7%), and the EU average of ~8%. The COVID pandemic exposed severe capacity shortfalls — Italy had one of the worst ICU-to-population ratios in the OECD. Post-pandemic, the 2024 state budget increased health appropriations modestly, but regional disparities remain stark: the North-South divide in healthcare quality is one of Italy's most persistent inequalities. Private health spending fills gaps, especially in diagnostics and specialist care in the South.

Healthcare: ~6.8% GDP · SSN universal but underfunded vs EU peers
🌑

The Shadow Economy: Italy's Structural Revenue Drag

Italy's shadow economy is estimated at approximately 11–12% of GDP by ISTAT, generating an annual tax gap of roughly €80–100 billion. This is reflected in Italy's tax wedge — one of the highest in the OECD — which incentivises informal work and undeclared income. Enforcement has improved through mandatory e-invoicing (Fattura Elettronica, introduced 2019 and extended to all suppliers), real-time transaction reporting, and digital payment incentives. VAT compliance has meaningfully improved since 2019. Italy's 42.8% tax-to-GDP ratio in 2024 (Wikipedia/Taxation in Italy) is high in absolute terms but would be even higher if informality were eliminated.

Shadow economy ~11–12% GDP · tax gap ~€80–100B annually
