China's four-budget system: This report covers the National General Public Budget (GPB) only — the primary fiscal account. China operates three additional budgets: the Government-Managed Funds budget (dominated by land-sale revenue, ¥7.3T in 2024, down 10.5%); the State Capital Operations budget (SOE dividends); and the Social Insurance Funds budget (pension & healthcare contributions). The official 3% deficit applies only to the GPB. Economists at PIIE estimate the broad consolidated deficit including all budgets and off-budget financing at approximately 6.5% of GDP in 2024. Revenue shown includes ¥2.54T in "supplementary resources" drawn from budget stabilization funds and carryovers.

Central vs. local spending: Of the ¥28.6T in total national GPB expenditure, only ¥4.1T was direct central government spending; the remaining ¥24.4T was local government spending, partially funded through central transfer payments of ¥10.0T. This extreme fiscal decentralization — where local governments execute ~85% of spending — is a defining structural feature of China's public finance. Education, healthcare, and social security are predominantly delivered and funded at the local level. Figures shown are consolidated national totals.
Context & explanations
🎓

Education: China's Largest Budget Line for a Decade

For over a decade, education has been the single largest line item in China's general public budget, reflecting the Communist Party's priority of building human capital. The ¥4.17T allocation covers compulsory education, rural school construction, vocational training, universities, and scholarship programs. Since 2012, the government has maintained a legal commitment to spend at least 4% of GDP on education across all budget levels. The 2024 figure represents about 2.7% of the ¥137T nominal GDP via the GPB alone, with the remainder of the 4% mandate fulfilled through local government own-source spending and the broader education surcharge system.

Education: 14.6% of all GPB spending
🌾

Agriculture Spending Surged 12.7% — a Record High

Spending on agriculture, forestry, and water conservancy jumped nearly 13% to ¥2.7T in 2024, the fastest growth of any major budget category and an all-time record. This surge reflected priority spending on rural revitalization programs, flood control after devastating summer floods in parts of northern China, irrigation upgrades, and food security investments. Beijing's food security anxiety has intensified: China's grain self-sufficiency strategy led to the world's largest state grain stockpiling program. The agricultural budget also funds rural road construction, clean water projects, and the ¥177B follow-up rural revitalization assistance fund targeting formerly impoverished counties.

Agriculture: +12.7% · All-time record ¥2.70T
💊

Healthcare Fell 9% — but This Is a COVID Normalization

Public health and sanitation expenditure fell sharply by over 9% to approximately ¥2.0T. This sounds alarming but is actually a statistical normalization: 2022 and 2023 saw massively elevated health spending due to COVID-19 testing, quarantine infrastructure, and vaccination campaigns. The 2024 figure of ¥2.0T closely mirrors pre-pandemic levels of ¥1.9T in 2020–2021. China's publicly funded healthcare is delivered through a layered system: the GPB funds public health programs and hospital subsidies, while a separate Social Insurance Funds budget (not shown here) covers the Basic Medical Insurance systems (Urban Employee BMI and Urban-Rural Resident BMI), which collectively cover over 1.3 billion people.

Health: ¥2.0T · COVID normalization, not a real cut
🛡️

Defence: Official 1.3% of GDP — But Actual Is Debated

China's official defence budget was ¥1.665T ($231B), a 7.2% increase for the ninth consecutive year of single-digit growth. The NPC budget report confirms 100% execution. At 1.3% of GDP, the official figure is below the global average. However, independent estimates diverge sharply. SIPRI estimates ~$309B for 2023 (the most recent year), while a 2024 RAND/Texas NSR study estimates 2024 actual defence spending at ~$471B (~27–33% above official figures), accounting for off-budget R&D, paramilitary (People's Armed Police), and other items. The US DoD's 2024 China Military Power Report suggests a 40–90% understatement. The true figure likely falls between $295B and $471B.

Official: ¥1.67T · SIPRI/RAND est: $295–471B total
💸

The Hidden Deficit: Land Sales Collapse & Broad Fiscal Pressure

The official 3% deficit masks China's true fiscal strain. The Government-Managed Funds budget — which relies on land-sale revenue — saw receipts fall 10.5% to ¥7.3T in 2024 as the property crisis continued, creating a ¥1.3T shortfall in that sub-budget alone. Additionally, the ¥1T in ultra-long special treasury bonds issued in 2024 does not count toward the official deficit. When all budget layers and off-budget borrowing are included, PIIE estimates the broad deficit at ~6.5% of GDP. Meanwhile, local governments carried hidden off-balance-sheet debt estimated at ¥50–65 trillion, with a landmark ¥2T debt-swap package announced in late 2024 to convert hidden local debt into official bonds.

Broad deficit: ~6.5% GDP · Land revenue –10.5%
🔬

Science & Technology: Strategic Priority with 6% Growth

Government spending on science and technology grew 6% to approximately ¥1.0T in 2024, reflecting Xi Jinping's "self-reliance in science and technology" agenda. When combined with R&D spending across all sectors (including state enterprise and private investment), China's total R&D expenditure reached ¥3.613T — or 2.68% of GDP — placing China second globally in absolute R&D spending behind the US. The central government's science budget weighted toward basic research and strategic technologies: semiconductors, AI, quantum computing, aerospace, and biotech. The 2025 budget raised central science spending another 10% to ¥398B, signaling continued escalation of the technology competition with the United States.

R&D: ¥3.61T total · 2.68% of GDP · World No. 2
