Context & explanations
💊

Novo Nordisk Effect: The Pharmaceutical Surge

The 4.5% GDP surplus in 2024 — the fourth consecutive year in positive territory — was partly driven by the extraordinary success of the Danish pharmaceutical sector, dominated by Novo Nordisk (Ozempic/Wegovy). Denmark's export revenues surged, lifting GDP and corporate tax receipts well above expectations. Corporation tax and the PAL pension-return tax together came in significantly higher than forecast. Novo Nordisk alone accounted for roughly 70% of GDP growth in H1 2024, making Denmark an outlier among European economies.

Corp. tax + PAL beat forecast by ~DKK 30B+
🏥

Healthcare: DKK 278B — Largest Budget Function

Total Danish healthcare spending reached DKK 278 billion in 2024, a 4% rise from 2023 — the first increase since 2021. Nearly half (DKK 128B, or 46%) was allocated to hospital services, including psychiatric care. Denmark's five administrative regions are responsible for delivering almost all hospital care, paid for by block grants from the state and co-payments from municipalities. Total health spending equals roughly 9.5% of GDP. Out-of-pocket payments cover mainly dental care and outpatient medicines, accounting for about 13% of total health expenditure.

Healthcare: ~9.5% of GDP · DKK 278B total
🎓

No Social Security Contributions — A Nordic Anomaly

Denmark is the only OECD country that raises zero revenue from social security contributions. The Danish welfare state — including healthcare, pensions, unemployment, and family benefits — is funded entirely from general income taxes and VAT. The 8% Labour Market Contribution (AM-bidrag) functions like a payroll tax but is legally classified as income tax. This makes Denmark's tax structure unusual: personal income tax (including AM-bidrag) accounts for well over 50% of total government revenue, far above the OECD average of ~25%.

No social security contributions: unique in OECD
💰

VAT at 25%: Joint-Highest in Europe

Denmark's 25% flat VAT rate (MOMS) is the joint-highest in the EU alongside Hungary and Croatia. Unlike most EU countries, Denmark applies virtually a single rate — there are no reduced rates on food, medicines, or children's clothing, only a zero rate for very limited categories (e.g. newspapers). This simplicity yields high compliance and revenue efficiency. VAT generated approximately DKK 280B in 2024, making it the largest single indirect tax. The absence of reduced rates means household consumption is taxed uniformly, which critics argue is regressive.

VAT 25% — no reduced rates · ~DKK 280B revenue
🛡️

Defence: Racing to Hit the 2% NATO Target

Denmark's Ministry of Defence Finance Act appropriation was DKK 36.2 billion in 2024 — but the broader NATO-defined defence expenditure figure including civil defence and security-related items in other ministries reached approximately DKK 71B (2.4% of GDP). Denmark committed to meeting the 2% NATO threshold after years below it, and the government's Fiscal and Structural Policy Plan explicitly mandated that defence and security expenditures reach at least 2% of GDP. Additional Ukraine military support reserves were held outside the regular defence budget.

NATO definition: ~2.4% GDP · DKK 71B+
👴

The Folkepension: A Universal, Tax-Funded State Pension

Denmark's public old-age pension (Folkepension) is a universal, residence-based scheme funded from general taxation — not contributions. A single pensioner can receive up to ~DKK 13,853/month before tax (basic amount + supplement). The statutory pension age is 67 and rising toward 68 in 2030, indexed to life expectancy. Total public pension expenditure is around 7–8% of GDP. Crucially, Denmark also has large occupational (2nd pillar) and voluntary private pension savings — Danes' private pension assets exceed 200% of GDP, reducing long-term pressure on the public scheme.

Folkepension: ~7–8% GDP · age 67 rising to 68
