Context & explanations
💸

Interest Payments: India's Largest Single Expenditure

At ₹11.63L cr, interest payments on government debt are India's single biggest budget line — 24% of all central government expenditure and 37% of revenue receipts. Every rupee borrowed today adds to this burden, which has nearly doubled in a decade as the debt stock swelled, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic. India's central government outstanding liabilities are estimated at 56.8% of GDP in FY2024-25. The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act targets reducing this ratio over time, and the fiscal deficit is being deliberately compressed — from 9.2% of GDP in FY2020-21 (COVID peak) to 4.9% in FY2024-25 — to slow the accumulation of new debt.

Interest: 24% of all spending · 37% of revenue receipts
🛣️

Infrastructure Push: ₹11.11L cr Capex — Record 3.4% of GDP

Capital expenditure of ₹11,11,111 crore (a distinctive symbolic figure) marks a third consecutive year of double-digit capex growth and is triple the level of FY2019-20. The bulk goes to roads (Ministry of Road Transport & Highways: ₹2.78L cr) and railways (₹2.55L cr), making them the second and third largest ministry allocations after defence. The government is funding 25,000 km of road construction annually, the world's largest rail expansion, new airports, and digital infrastructure. An additional ₹1.5L cr in interest-free 50-year loans to states further amplifies infrastructure investment at the sub-national level. Research shows each ₹1 of public capex generates ₹2.5–3 in GDP over five years.

Capex: ₹11.11L cr · 3.4% GDP · Triple FY2019-20 level
🛡️

Defence: ₹6.22L cr — Largest Ministry Allocation for Second Year

The Ministry of Defence received ₹6.22L cr, the highest allocation of any central ministry at 13% of total expenditure. The budget reflects India's determination to modernise the armed forces — around 27% goes to capital outlay for weapons and platforms, while ~53% covers revenue (salaries and operations) and 20% defence pensions. India is now the world's third-largest military spender in absolute terms. Under the "Aatmanirbhar Bharat" (self-reliant India) defence initiative, a growing share of procurement is reserved for domestic industry — a ring-fenced domestic procurement budget (iDEX) now sits at ₹1,48,000 crore. India is simultaneously managing border tensions with both China and Pakistan, driving sustained modernisation.

Defence: ₹6.22L cr · 13% of budget · ~2% of GDP
🌾

Subsidies: ₹4.38L cr — Food & Fertiliser Dominate

India's subsidy bill of ₹4.38L cr (9.1% of total spending) covers food, fertiliser, and other schemes. The food subsidy of ₹2.05L cr funds the National Food Security Act, providing 5 kg of free grain monthly to 800 million beneficiaries — the world's largest food security scheme, extended free of cost until 2028. Fertiliser subsidies (₹1.64L cr) keep agricultural input costs low for 140 million farm households. Both have been declining as a share of GDP since the COVID surge, reflecting gradual fiscal consolidation. PM-KISAN transfers ₹6,000/year directly to ~110 million farmers, adding ₹0.20L cr to rural transfers. MGNREGS employment guarantee received ₹0.86L cr, unchanged from last year.

Food subsidy: ₹2.05L cr · Free grain to 800M people
📊

Income Tax Now Bigger Than Corporate Tax

For the first time in recent memory, personal income tax (PIT) collections (₹11.87L cr budgeted; ₹12.90L cr actual per CBDT) have overtaken corporate tax (₹10.20L cr budgeted; ₹12.40L cr actual). PIT now accounts for ~31% of Gross Tax Revenue vs. ~27% for corporate tax. This reflects the 2019 corporate tax rate cut (from 30% to 22%) which permanently reduced the corporate share, and rapid formalisation of the economy bringing more salaried workers into the tax net. GST (Central component: ₹9.11L cr) continues its strong post-pandemic recovery. At ₹38.40L cr, India's Gross Tax Revenue-to-GDP ratio is approximately 11.7% — well below the OECD average of ~34%, indicating substantial scope for future revenue expansion as formalisation deepens.

PIT overtakes corporate tax · Tax-to-GDP: 11.7%
🏥

Health & Education: Low Shares Reflect India's Federal Structure

Health (₹0.90L cr) and Education (₹1.25L cr) together account for just 4.4% of the Union Budget, which seems remarkably low for a country of 1.4 billion people. This is misleading: India is a federal republic where health and education are primarily state subjects. State governments spend approximately 3–4× as much as the Centre on each. India's combined (centre + state) public health spending is around 2.1% of GDP, and education around 4.6% of GDP — closer to peer country averages when both tiers are counted. The Union Budget's Ayushman Bharat health insurance program alone covers 550 million people. Union health spending is focused on national programs like National Health Mission, AIIMS hospitals, and vaccines.

State governments spend 3–4× more than Centre on health/education
