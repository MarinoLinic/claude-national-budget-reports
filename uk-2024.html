Context & explanations
🏥

The NHS: The Dominant Spending Item

At ~£185B for health in total (NHS England ~£177B plus devolved nations' health budgets), healthcare is by far the single largest expenditure function — around 1 in every 6 pounds of all public spending. The NHS is free at the point of use, funded entirely from general taxation with no insurance premium. This is a fundamental structural difference from the US system, where public health spending is split between Medicare and Medicaid and covers far fewer people.

NHS: ~£177B for England alone
👵

State Pension: The Largest Single Benefit

The State Pension cost £124B in 2023-24, making it the single largest welfare payment. The "triple lock" guarantee (rises by whichever is highest: inflation, earnings, or 2.5%) has driven sustained above-inflation increases. The full new State Pension is £221.20/week (from April 2024). Unlike Croatia's two-pillar system or the US OASDI, the UK pension is flat-rate and entirely PAYG — there is no funded second pillar in the mandatory public scheme.

Triple lock · £124B · flat-rate PAYG
📊

Debt Interest: £110B After RPI-Linked Gilt Crisis

Debt interest jumped from £43B in 2018-19 to ~£110B in 2023-24. The UK has a large proportion of index-linked gilts (tied to RPI inflation), which became extremely expensive when inflation hit 11%. The 2022 Kwarteng mini-budget also briefly spiked gilt yields. By 2023-24, debt interest had stabilised but remained at ~10% of all spending — double its pre-pandemic share.

£110B · 10% of spending · RPI-linked
💼

NICs: A Tax in All But Name

National Insurance Contributions (£177.7B) are nominally a social insurance contribution but are not hypothecated — they go into the same pot as income tax and fund general spending. The UK raises relatively little from NICs as a share of GDP compared to continental Europe (Croatia: 8.5% of GDP; UK: ~6.5% of GDP; France: ~16% of GDP). NICs create a "wedge" between employment and self-employment that critics say distorts the labour market.

£177.7B · not hypothecated · 6.5% GDP
🏘️

Universal Credit: One Benefit to Rule Them All

Universal Credit (£51B of the £81B working-age welfare total) is replacing six legacy means-tested benefits: income-based JSA, income-related ESA, Income Support, Child Tax Credit, Working Tax Credit, and Housing Benefit. The rollout is now ~95% complete. UC was designed to make work pay by tapering benefits at 55p for every £1 earned. Disability benefits (£35B) remain separate, as does Child Benefit (£13B), which is universal.

UC: £51B · 6 benefits merged into 1
🗳️

Devolved Spending & the Barnett Formula

Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland receive block grants calculated by the Barnett Formula — roughly a population-weighted share of changes in English departmental spending. The grants (~£45B combined in 2023-24) are shown within education, health, and other functional categories, not as a separate line. Scotland receives ~£14,400 per head vs ~£12,900 for England. The devolved nations control how they spend their block grants within devolved competencies.

Barnett Formula · ~£45B block grants
