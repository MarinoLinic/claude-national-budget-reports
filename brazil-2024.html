Context & explanations
👴

Pensions Dominate Federal Spending

The INSS (RGPS) pays out roughly R$870B annually to over 40 million active beneficiaries — the single largest line in the federal budget at ~8.5% of GDP. The 2019 pension reform (Bolsonaro/Guedes) raised minimum retirement ages and tightened contribution rules to slow the growth of this expenditure, projecting R$800B in 10-year savings. Civil servant pensions (RPPS) add another ~R$120B.

Pension total: ~9.7% GDP
💸

Interest Payments: A Crushing Burden

Brazil's Selic policy rate — at 10.5% through much of 2024 — generates enormous interest costs on ~R$7.1 trillion in public debt. Nominal interest payments of ~R$855B (7.3% of GDP) dwarf those of any comparably-sized economy. The gap between the primary deficit (~1.2% GDP) and nominal deficit (~8.5% GDP) is almost entirely explained by debt servicing. Market concerns about fiscal slippage pushed the Selic back above 12% by year-end.

Debt interest: ~7.3% GDP
🌱

Bolsa Família — World's Largest Cash Transfer

Bolsa Família was relaunched by Lula in 2023 with expanded benefits, serving over 21 million families (~80 million individuals, ~40% of Brazil's population). Average transfer ~R$672/month. The programme costs ~R$168B annually (1.6% of GDP) and includes supplements of R$150/child under 6 and R$50/pregnant woman. Despite its scale, it is far smaller than the pension system and targeted at Brazil's poorest.

Bolsa Família: ~1.6% GDP
🏥

SUS — Universal Healthcare Under Pressure

Brazil's Sistema Único de Saúde (SUS) guarantees free universal healthcare to 215 million people. Federal health spending is constitutionally mandated at a minimum percentage of net revenue. Spending reached approximately R$210B (2.0% of GDP) in 2024, supplemented by state and municipal contributions. The SUS faces chronic underfunding relative to demand, particularly post-pandemic, driving debate about increasing the federal minimum allocation.

Federal health: ~2.0% GDP
🧾

Record Tax Revenue — Still Not Enough

Federal tax collection hit R$2.709 trillion in 2024 — a historical record representing real growth of 9.6%. Growth was driven by economic expansion (GDP +3.1%), PIS/COFINS from fuel taxation reinstatement, and aggressive enforcement. Despite record revenues, mandatory spending grew faster. Brazil's tax burden (~33% of GDP) is the highest in Latin America and comparable to many European countries, yet large deficits persist due to structural spending commitments.

Tax revenue record: R$2.71T
⚖️

New Fiscal Framework — Zero Primary Target

Lula's 2023 fiscal framework replaced Temer's spending ceiling with a rule limiting real spending growth to 70% of real revenue growth, capped at 2.5% p.a. The primary deficit target for 2024 was zero (±0.25% GDP tolerance). The actual primary deficit came in at ~1.2% GDP — a miss. This triggered market concern, BRL depreciation to ~R$6/USD, and prompted late-year spending freeze announcements of R$15B by Minister Haddad.

Primary deficit: ~1.2% GDP (target: 0%)
