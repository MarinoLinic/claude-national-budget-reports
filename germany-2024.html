Context & explanations
🔒

The Schuldenbremse — Germany's Debt Brake

Since 2009, Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) restricts the federal structural deficit to 0.35% of GDP (the Schuldenbremse). Länder are held to zero structural deficits. After suspension during 2020–23 (pandemic + energy crisis), the brake was reinstated for 2024. A landmark Federal Constitutional Court ruling in November 2023 struck down a €60B fund transfer, forcing painful mid-year cuts. The Bundesverfassungsgericht ruling effectively ended the Scholz coalition and triggered early elections in early 2025.

Structural deficit limit: 0.35% GDP
👴

Statutory Pension Insurance (GRV) — The Largest Line

Germany's Gesetzliche Rentenversicherung paid ~€390B to approximately 21 million pensioners in 2024 — the single biggest expenditure in the general government budget at ~9.2% of GDP. The contribution rate is 18.6% of gross wages (split 50/50 employer/employee). Federal budget transfers of ~€100B per year bridge the structural shortfall. Pension indexation follows gross wage growth, and the pension level is protected at a minimum 48% replacement rate under Scholz-era legislation.

GRV pension spending: ~9.2% GDP
🏥

Statutory Health Insurance (GKV) — Universal Coverage

Germany's Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung covers ~90% of the population (the rest use private insurance, PKV). Health spending reached ~€310B in 2024, rising 8% as hospital costs, medication and nursing care expenditure increased sharply. The GKV is financed by a base contribution of 14.6% of wages (employee/employer split), plus individual additional rates set by each Krankenkasse (averaging ~1.7% in 2024). Long-term care insurance (GPV) added a further ~€65B in 2024.

GKV + GPV: ~8.8% GDP
🛡️

Defence: Racing to Meet the NATO 2% Target

Germany's 2024 defence spending — combining the core Bundeswehr budget (~€51.8B) with the €100B Sondervermögen special fund (allocated over several years) — reached approximately €90B, or ~2.1% of GDP. This was Germany's first sustained commitment to the NATO 2% target since reunification. Russia's war in Ukraine transformed German defence policy (Zeitenwende). The special fund, created in 2022, is expected to be fully disbursed by ~2027, after which baseline spending will need to remain above 2% via the regular budget.

Defence: ~2.1% GDP (NATO target met)
💶

VAT at 19% — Lower Than EU Peers

Germany's standard VAT rate of 19% (reduced: 7% for food, books, public transport) generated €302B in 2024 — the largest single tax line. Despite being Europe's largest economy, Germany's VAT revenues as a share of GDP are below the OECD median, partly due to the generous reduced-rate system. The IMF has repeatedly flagged this gap. Income and social contribution revenues, by contrast, significantly exceed peer-country averages, reflecting Germany's high-wage, high-social-contribution employment model.

VAT revenue: €302B · 7.1% GDP
🌍

Bürgergeld & Migration — A Contested Budget Line

The Bürgergeld (citizens' benefit, replacing Hartz IV) provides means-tested income support and covered ~5.5 million recipients in 2024 at a cost of ~€45B, up €2.4B year-on-year. A significant share of recipients are migrants and asylum seekers, making it a lightning-rod political issue that helped fuel the CDU/CSU election campaign. Integration costs — language courses, youth welfare, housing assistance, integration allowances — added a further ~€30B. The Ampel coalition's collapse was partly driven by FDP demands to cut Bürgergeld as a condition of the 2025 budget agreement.

Bürgergeld: ~€45B · ~1.1% GDP
