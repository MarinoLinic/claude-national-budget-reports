Context & explanations
🪖

Defence Spending: A Post-Soviet Record at ~6.6% of GDP

National Defence consumed an estimated ₽13.2 trillion in 2024 — roughly 32.5% of all federal spending and the single largest budget item, surpassing social policy for the first time in post-Soviet history. This represents an almost 70% increase from the planned 2023 level in nominal terms. SIPRI estimates total Russian military expenditure including classified items at around 7.1% of GDP in 2024, compared to 3–4% pre-invasion. Nearly 85% of defence chapter spending is classified, making precise verification impossible. Actual spending exceeded the originally approved ₽10.8T by some ₽2.4T, a pattern seen each year since 2022.

Defence: ~32.5% of all federal spending
🏦

VAT Overtakes Oil & Gas as Top Revenue Source

For the first time, value-added tax (₽13.5T, +16% year-on-year) eclipsed oil and gas revenues (₽11.1T) as the federal budget's largest income line. This reflects both the wartime expansion of the domestic economy (GDP grew 4.1% in real terms) and the ruble's depreciation boosting nominal ruble values of all transactions. Oil and gas still accounted for 30.3% of total revenues — well above the EU average for comparable economies, but historically low for Russia. Baseline oil revenues assumed a $60/bbl Urals price; actual Urals averaged ~$68–70/bbl in 2024, yielding ₽1.3T in additional windfall revenues above baseline.

Non-oil revenues: 70% of total · First in post-Soviet history
📉

Deficit Tripled vs Plan: December Surge

The initial 2024 budget law targeted a deficit of ₽1.6T (0.9% of GDP). The actual deficit of ₽3.5T (1.7% of GDP) was more than double. The overshoot was almost entirely explained by a ₽3.1–3.4T December spending surge — the Finance Ministry pre-paid 2025 obligations using windfall oil revenues, providing budgetary flexibility for 2025. Through the first 11 months of 2024, the deficit was only ₽0.4T. The deficit was financed through OFZ domestic bond issuance (~₽4.1T planned, expensive at 16–21% interest rates) and drawdowns from the National Wealth Fund (~₽1.3T). Russia's total public debt remains modest at around 18–19% of GDP.

Deficit: ₽3.5T · 1.7% of GDP · 2.2× initial plan
💊

Healthcare & Education: Real-Term Cuts Despite Nominal Stability

While healthcare (₽1.6T) and education (₽1.5T) held roughly steady in nominal terms compared to 2023, their shares of total budget fell — from ~5.2% to ~4.4% for healthcare, and ~4.8% to ~4.2% for education. With CPI inflation reaching close to 10% by end-2024 and the budget growing by 24%, this amounts to a significant real-term reduction in civilian social investment. Both functions are predominantly funded by regional governments and extra-budgetary healthcare funds (OMC system), so federal figures understate total public health and education spending, which is closer to 4–5% of GDP when all tiers are included.

Healthcare + Education: 7.7% of federal outlays combined
💸

Debt Service Costs Surging at 21% Key Rate

Debt service consumed approximately ₽2.3T in 2024 (5.8% of all outlays), nearly double its 2019 share. Russia's central bank raised its key rate to a historic 21% in October 2024 in an attempt to curb inflation that approached 10% year-on-year. Around 50% of Russia's domestic OFZ bonds carry floating interest rates tied to the key rate, meaning the cost of servicing existing debt rises with every rate hike. The Finance Ministry estimated debt service would rise to 7.7% of outlays in 2025. Unlike pre-invasion years, Russia can no longer access international bond markets due to sanctions, making the domestic bond market — dominated by state-controlled banks — the only borrowing channel.

Debt service: 5.8% of outlays · Doubling by 2025
🏗️

National Economy: Crowded Out by War Spending

Spending on the national economy (infrastructure, industry support, agriculture, national projects) fell from ~14% of total outlays in 2023 to around 11% in 2024 — approximately ₽4.4T. This chapter funds roads, railways, digital infrastructure, special economic zones, import-substitution programs, and state development bank capitalizations. The decline reflects the explicit trade-off Putin's government made: with total spending constrained by fiscal rules, the defence buildup squeezed out civilian infrastructure investment. Russia's GDP still grew 4.1% in 2024 largely due to wartime industrial demand rather than productivity-enhancing investment.

National economy: fell from 14% → 11% of total outlays
